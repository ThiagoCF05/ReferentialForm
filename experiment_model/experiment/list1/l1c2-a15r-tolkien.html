J. R. R. Tolkien

He CBE (3 January 1892 – 2 September 1973) was a British writer and university professor who is best known as the author of The Hobbit and The Lord of the Rings. This British writer was an Oxford professor of Anglo-Saxon language (1925 to 1945) and English language and literature (1945 to 1959). This British writer was a strongly committed Roman Catholic. This British writer was a close friend of C. S. Lewis; they were both members of the informal literary discussion group known as the Inklings.

In addition to The Hobbit and The Lord of the Rings, this British writer's published fiction includes The Silmarillion and other posthumously published books about what he called a legendarium, a connected body of tales, fictional histories, invented languages, and other literary essays about an imagined world called Arda, and Middle-earth (derived from middangeard, the lands inhabitable by Men) in particular, loosely identified as an "alternative" remote past of our own world. Most of these works were compiled from the British writer's notes by his son Christopher Tolkien.

The enduring popularity and influence of the British writer's works have led to him being popularly identified as the "father of modern fantasy literature"; though this title is strictly inaccurate since there were predecessors such as William Morris, Robert E. Howard and E. R. Eddison, the British writer did have an indisputable and lasting effect on the field and related media. Many fantasy settings like Dungeons and Dragons and Warcraft owe something, directly or indirectly, to him .

His other published fiction includes stories not directly related to the legendarium, some of them originally told to this British writer's children.
