Guyana

The country (pronounced [IPA: [gaɪ'a.na]], occasionally Anglicized as [IPA: [gaɪ'æ.nə]]) is the only nation state of the Commonwealth of Nations on the mainland of South America. It is north of the Equator but in the tropics and _ has an atlantic coast. This country is bordered to the east by Suriname, to the south and southwest by Brazil and to the west by Venezuela. The country is the third smallest country on the mainland of South America and approximately the size of Great Britain. Guyana is the only South American country whose official language is English, and _ is one of only two remaining countries in the mainland Americas to drive on the left.

The official name of this country is the Co-operative Republic of Guyana. Guyana is an Amerindian word meaning Land of Many Waters. Guyana can be characterized by the country's vast rain forests dissected by numerous rivers, creeks and waterfalls, notably Kaieteur Falls on the Potaro River. Guyana's tepuis are famous for being the inspiration for Sir Arthur Conan Doyle's 1912 novel The Lost World. Guyana enjoys a friendly, multicultural society, high floral and faunal biodiversity, prize-winning rum, British Colonial architecture and Demerara sugar.

Though physically part of South America, culturally this country is Caribbean rather than Latin American and it is considered part of the West Indies. Other languages of Guyana include Creolese, Hindi, Wai-Wai, Arawak and Macushi.

This country is currently in a border dispute with Suriname, which claims the land east of the Corentyne River in southeastern Guyana. Venezuela claims the land west of the Essequibo River as part of Guayana Esequiba.
