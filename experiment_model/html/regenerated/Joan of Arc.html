Joan of Arc

Joan of Arc , also known as Jeanne d'Arc, (c.1412 - 30 May 1431) was a national heroine of France and _ is a saint of the Roman Catholic Church. She asserted that she had visions from God which told her to recover her homeland from English domination late in the Hundred Years' War. The uncrowned King Charles VII sent her to the siege at Orléans as part of a relief mission. She gained prominence when she overcame the light regard of veteran commanders and _ lifted the siege in only nine days. Several more swift victories led to Charles VII's coronation at Reims and settled the disputed succession to the throne.

The renewed French confidence outlasted Joan of Arc's own brief career. Joan of Arc refused to leave the field when she was wounded during an attempt to recapture Paris that autumn. Hampered by court intrigues, Joan of Arc led only minor companies from then onward and _ fell prisoner at a skirmish near Compiègne the following spring. A politically motivated trial convicted her of heresy. The English regent John of Lancaster, 1st Duke of Bedford had the national heroine burnt at the stake in Rouen. She had been the heroine of her country at the age of seventeen and _ died at just nineteen. Some twenty-four years later Pope Callixtus III reopened the case and a new finding overturned the original conviction. Her piety to the end impressed the retrial court. Pope Benedict XV canonized Joan of Arc on 16 May, 1920.

Joan of Arc has remained an important figure in Western culture. From Napoleon to the present, French politicians of all leanings have invoked her memory. Major writers and composers who have created works about her include Shakespeare, Voltaire, Schiller, Verdi, Tchaikovsky, Twain, Shaw, Brecht and Honegger. Depictions of Joan of Arc continue in film, television, and song.
