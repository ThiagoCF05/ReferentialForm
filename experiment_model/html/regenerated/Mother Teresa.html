Mother Teresa

Mother Teresa (born Agnes Gonxha Bojaxhiu (August 27, 1910 – September 5, 1997), Bharat Ratna, OM, was an Albanian Roman Catholic nun who founded the Missionaries of Charity in India. Her work among the poverty-stricken in Kolkata (Calcutta) made her one of the world's most famous people, and she was beatified by Pope John Paul II in October 2003. Hence, she may be properly called Blessed Teresa by Catholics.

Born in Skopje, Ottoman Empire (located in modern-day Macedonia), at 18 Teresa left home to join the Sisters of Loretto. In 1962, Teresa received the Magsaysay Award for Peace and International Understanding. In 1971, she was awarded the Pope John XXIII Peace Prize. She was also awarded the Templeton Prize in 1973, the Nobel Peace Prize in 1979, and India's highest civilian award, the Bharat Ratna, in 1980. The Albanian Roman Catholic nun was awarded the Legion d'Honneur from Jean-Claude Duvalier in 1981. She was presented with the Presidential Medal of Freedom in 1985, _ was made an Honorary Citizen of the United States (one of only two people to have this honor during their lifetime) in 1996, and _ received the Congressional Gold Medal in 1997. Teresa was the first and only person to be featured on an Indian postage stamp while still alive.

While Teresa's supporters sometimes referred to her as the "Angel of Mercy" and "Saint of the Gutter," critics have raised questions about her public statements, working practices, political connections, and the use of funds donated to her charity.
