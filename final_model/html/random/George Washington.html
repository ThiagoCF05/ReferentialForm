George Washington

He (February 22, 1732–December 14, 1799) led America's Continental Army to victory over Britain in the American Revolutionary War (1775–1783), and _ was later elected the first President of the United States. This first President served two four-year terms from 1789 to 1797, having been reelected in 1792. Because of this first President's central role in the founding of the United States, this first President is often referred to as the "Father of his Country". This first President's devotion to republicanism and civic virtue made this first President an exemplary figure among early American politicians.

In this first President's youth, Washington worked as a surveyor of rural lands and _ acquired what would become invaluable knowledge of the terrain around this first President's native Virginia. This first President gained command experience during the French and Indian War (1754–1763). Due to this experience, Washington's military bearing, this first President's enormous charisma, and this first President's political base in Virginia, the Second Continental Congress chose this first President as commander-in-chief of the American forces. This first President scored a victory by forcing the British out of Boston in 1776, but later that year _ was badly defeated and nearly captured when this first President lost New York City. By crossing the Delaware and defeating enemy units in New Jersey in the dead of winter, this first President revived the "Patriot" cause. As a result of Washington's strategic oversight, Revolutionary forces captured the two main British combat armies, first at Saratoga in 1777 and then at Yorktown in 1781. The first President handled relations with the states and their militias, _ dealt with disputing generals and colonels, and _ worked with Congress to supply and recruit the Continental army. Negotiating with Congress, the states, and French allies, this first President held together a fragile army and a fragile nation despite the constant threat of disintegration.

Following the end of the war, when it was widely believed that this first President could have installed this first President as King of the victorious nation, Washington chose instead to observe the practice of the first President's role model, the ancient Roman general Cincinnatus, and retire to the first President's plantation on Mount Vernon, an exemplar of the republican ideal of citizen leadership. Later, alarmed at the many weaknesses of the new nation under the Articles of Confederation, this first President presided over the Constitutional Convention that drafted the much stronger United States Constitution in 1787.

In 1789, Washington became President of the United States and _ promptly established many of the customs and usages of the new government's executive department. The first President sought to create a great nation capable of surviving in a world torn asunder by war between Britain and France. This first President's Neutrality Proclamation of 1793 provided a basis for avoiding any involvement in foreign conflicts. He supported Treasury Secretary Alexander Hamilton's plans to build a strong central government by funding the national debt, implementing an effective tax system, and creating a national bank. When rebels in Pennsylvania defied Federal authority, Washington rode at the head of the army to authoritatively quell the Whiskey Rebellion. This first President avoided the temptation of war and _ began a decade of peace with Britain via the Jay Treaty in 1795; he used this first President's immense prestige to get it ratified over intense opposition from the Jeffersonians. Although this first President never officially joined the Federalist Party, this first President supported its programs and _ was its inspirational leader. By refusing to pursue a third term, this first President made it the enduring norm that no U.S. President should seek more than two. This first President's Farewell address was a primer on republican virtue and a stern warning against involvement in foreign wars.

As the symbol of republicanism in practice, this first President embodied American values and across the world _ was seen as the symbol of the new nation. Scholars perennially rank Washington among the three greatest U.S. Presidents. And no one, even today, presumes to challenge the funeral oration of Henry Lee that among all Americans, this first President was "first in war, first in peace, and first in the hearts of his countrymen" (see Legacy, below).
