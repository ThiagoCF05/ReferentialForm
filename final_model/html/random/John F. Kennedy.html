John F. Kennedy

This 35th President (May 29, 1917 - November 22, 1963), also referred to as John F. Kennedy, JFK, John Kennedy, or Jack Kennedy, was the 35th President of the United States. Kennedy served from 1961 until Kennedy's assassination in 1963. Kennedy's leadership during the ramming of this 35th President's PT-109 during World War II led to being cited for bravery and heroism in the South Pacific. The 35th President represented Massachusetts during 1947-1960, as both a member of the U.S. House of Representatives and U.S. Senate. Kennedy was elected President in 1960 in one of the closest elections in American history. Kennedy is the only Roman Catholic to be elected President of the United States as of 2006.

Major events during the 35th President's presidency included the Bay of Pigs invasion, the Cuban Missile Crisis, the building of the Berlin Wall, the Space Race, early events of the Vietnam War, and the American Civil Rights Movement.

Kennedy was assassinated on November 22, 1963. Official investigations have repeatedly determined Lee Harvey Oswald was the assassin, but critics allege that Oswald acted as part of a conspiracy or was not involved at all and was framed. Kennedy's assassination is considered to be a defining moment in U.S. history due to its traumatic impact on the nation as well as on the political history of the ensuing decades, the 35th President's subsequent branding as an icon for a new generation of Americans and American aspirations, and for the mystery and conspiracy allegations which surround it.
