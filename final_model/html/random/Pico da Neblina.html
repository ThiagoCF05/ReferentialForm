Pico da Neblina

It is the highest mountain in Brazil, 2,994 metres (9,822 feet) above sea level, at the Imeri mountain range (Serra do Imeri), which borders on Venezuela. As determined by a border survey expedition in 1962, this mountain's summit lies barely within Brazilian territory, being a mere 687 metres (2,254 feet) from the Venezuelan border at Pico 31 de Março. The latter is only 21 metres lower, Brazil's second highest mountain and the highest mountain in Venezuela outside of the Andes.

The mountain is often misquoted as being on the border between the two countries, and when one considers Pico da Neblina's massif as a whole (not just the main summit), this is really true.

It is also the highest point in the Guiana Shield, but unlike famous Monte Roraima, this mountain is not a tepui, as the typical and unique tabletop mountains of the Guiana Highlands are known. Tepuis are only found far to the northeast of the Imeri range. Furthermore, this mountain is not table-shaped, but a rather sharp peak.

The word neblina means "fog" in both Portuguese and Spanish; so, Pico da Neblina's name (meaning "Peak of the Mists") reflects the fact that the mountain is shrouded in dense clouds most of the time. Pico da Neblina was first ascended in 1965 by members of a Brazilian army expedition.

This mountain is contained in the Brazilian Pico da Neblina National Park; Pico da Neblina's northern slopes are also protected in Venezuela's Serranía de la Neblina National Park. The twin parks, together with the neighbouring Parima-Tapirapecó National Park (Venezuela), form a protected area complex of about 80,000 km², possibly the largest national park system in tropical rainforests in the world.
